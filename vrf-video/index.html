§ 01 — Goals

Four requirements for onchain randomness

Any usable source of randomness on a blockchain must satisfy all four at once.

01
Unbiasable
No participant — not even the operator — can tilt the outcome to their advantage.
02
Reliable
The value is produced at the promised time, even under adverse conditions.
03
Verifiable
Anyone can check the result against a public algorithm or cryptographic proof.
04
Unpredictable
No one learns the value ahead of time — or they could front-run for profit.
Four requirements: unbiasable, reliable, verifiable, unpredictable. Every usable source of onchain randomness must satisfy all four.
§ 02 — L1 Randomness

Three cryptographic building blocks

L1 chains compose these three primitives in different ways, depending on consensus design.

PRIMITIVE · PRIMARY
VRF
A secret-key holder produces a pseudo-random value. Anyone can verify it with the public key. Same input → same output.
PROTOCOL · PRIMARY
DKG
Distributed key generation splits one secret across many participants. A threshold must cooperate to use the key.
SIGNATURE · SUPPORTING
BLS
Signatures aggregate into one. This makes threshold signing efficient — the supporting tool for DKG.
VRF and DKG are the primary building blocks. BLS is what makes threshold signing practical.
L1 chains compose VRF, DKG and BLS signatures to build randomness. VRF and DKG are primary. BLS makes threshold signing efficient.
§ 03 — L2 Randomness

On L2, the validator structure is different

Techniques that rely on broad participation no longer hold when one actor sequences transactions.

L1

Hundreds of independent validators

L2

Often a single sequencer
SEQUENCER
one operator — or a small group
L1 has many validators. Most L2s run one sequencer, or a few operators. The anti-collusion assumption that makes DKG safe no longer holds.

§ 06 — Isolating the Secret Key

Three ways to isolate the key

The goal: generate randomness safely even when the sequencer must not learn the secret.

APPROACH · HARDWARE
TEE
A hardware-isolated enclave. The VRF key is created and used inside. Even the operator cannot read it.
APPROACH · SEQUENTIAL COMPUTATION
VDF
A function that must be computed step by step. The output only appears after real time has passed.
APPROACH · EXTERNAL BEACON
drand
Decentralized randomness beacon. The sequencer cannot access the beacon's secret key.
Three approaches to keep sk away from the sequencer. TEE enclave, sequential VDF, or an external beacon like drand.
§ 07 — Trade-offs

Each approach has a limit

No option is free — the question is which cost fits an L2 with fast block production.

TEE
Trusts the hardware.
You depend on the manufacturer to build the enclave correctly and keep its root keys safe.
VDF
ASICs. Delay.
Specialised hardware may compute faster than expected — and the extra time hurts fast finality.
BEACON
Fixed schedule.
drand outputs every 30 seconds — which may not match an L2 block cadence.
TEE trusts hardware. VDFs face ASICs and add latency. Beacons run on fixed schedules that may not match L2 block time.
